    About

    USC Gould is one of the nation's top-ranked law schools with a 115+ year history and reputation for academic excellence. We are known for our diverse student body, interdisciplinary education and our tight-knit alumni network of over 10,000 professionals throughout the globe. With entering J.D. class sizes of less than 200 the learning experience is intimate and collegial. Our location in Los Angeles provides unlimited opportunities in one of world's top legal markets. It is home to major entertainment, business and law firms, as well as a gateway to international markets.

    Student Life

    From your first day on campus you'll notice USC Gould is different. It's small enough to be a community in the real sense of the word even though USC is one the country's largest research universities, and located in the second largest city in the United States. People know your name here, faculty and administration are accessible, and your success is everyone's priority - all of which makes for an unparalleled student experience.

Taxation

Back to Course Listings

Course Number: LAW-600

Credits: 4

Course Description:

An introduction to the basic principles, policies, and provisions of the federal personal income tax. The course is designed both to present the fundamentals of taxation and to provide a foundation for those students who wish to take advanced tax courses. The course emphasizes both a close reading of the complex statute and the tensions that then follow from decades of judge-made interpretative rules unique to the area.

The income tax course should be considered even by students with no native interest in the subject, for several reasons. First, there is utility to gaining experience in reading complex statutory drafting – and there is no better example of that than the Internal Revenue Code. Second, the tension between the words of the Code and the efforts of judges to do justice, by their lights, is inherently interesting. Third, tax considerations are woven deeply into every commercial decision, and many personal ones. It is difficult to be a lawyer in private practice in any capacity without some familiarity with tax law modes of thought and basic rules. Fourth, the Internal Revenue Code is the principal income and wealth redistribution mechanism in the United States, as well as a vehicle for much hidden spending; its distributional and allocative consequences thus are important to the entire economy.

This year the course will spend less time on the policy implications of the income tax, for the simple reason that the school will offer a separate tax policy seminar in the Spring. LAW-600 is a prerequisite to that seminar.

Participation points will not be awarded.  Class is available for CR/D/F grading. PCs with SofTest will be allowed on the in-house final exam. 

Special Note: My book, We Are Better Than This: How Government Should Spend Our Money, will be published in Fall 2014. Its publication will require me to travel from time to time in connection with the publisher’s promotional efforts. As a result, we will have several makeup classes over the course of semester on Tuesdays at 12:00pm. All classes, including makeup classes, will be recorded and made generally available to students. I appreciate your understanding.

 

The Meaning of My Life, And Its Implications for You

So, I wrote a book, and to my amazement, it will be officially published By Oxford U. Press on October 1st, although it will ship from the internet resellers earlier than that.

It's titled We Are Better Than This: How Government Should Spend Our Money.  The intended audience is you, and others like you -- intelligent folk interested in why our fiscal policy (government spending and taxing) discourse is so screwed up, and how fiscal policy can be applied to increase the happiness of society. It is earnest and accessible, and only partly about the tax side of things. I've attached a one-page summary I cooked up.

It's available for pre-order from B&N or Amazon, and I will be thrilled to autograph all the copies you choose to purchase. Reading it will NOT help you on the final exam for Tax 1, but it will in my estimation make you an infinitely better person.

Exhibit A: Your Holiday Shopping, Simplified

 

Now, you may ask, if the book won’t be on the exam, what does this all mean for me? The consequence of my writing a book for which the publisher has expressed a certain amount of optimism, and which is aimed a broader than tax-nerd audience, is that I will have to travel back East for interviews, meetings with press/bloggers, and book events organized around my book. I've already done five long days of this in June, but it turns out that lots of folk (e.g. some radio shows) want their interviewee in person in their studio, and of course don't record their show until the book is published. So I have no choice but to go East and flog, flog, flog. On top of that, some august institutions have scheduled book events around my book, I suppose because they've run out of better ideas of what to do.

I am very sorry that this imposes a burden on all of you. There just doesn't seem to be any way around it, in light of how the publishing industry works, and where the media centers are located.

At a minimum, you should expect that I will be traveling the week of September 23rd and the week of October 27th.

I've arranged to hold make-up classes in general on Tuesdays at 12noon to 1:50pm. My proposal is to grit our teeth and jump into it, with make-up classes on:

August 26
Sept 9
Sept 16
Sept 23
Oct 21

That should do it for the semester, I expect, and the last may not be necessary. Of course, if Stephen Colbert calls up . . . .

All of my classes, including make-up classes, are routinely recorded and made available to students, which I hope will help you in juggling these make-up classes.

Again, I apologize. It's not fun to have Tax three days in a row, but on the other hand, you'll have two weeks off to chill, or whatever it is that students do.

Thank you for understanding.

Ed Kleinbard

 

We Are Better Than This fundamentally reframes budget debates in the United States, by demonstrating how government enhances what Adam Smith called “the happiness of society,”and what economists today call “welfare,”through investments that complement private enterprise, and through social insurance against the worst vicissitudes of life. The book thus is a principled and carefully argued call for the reinvigoration of government as a positive complement to private enterprise in contemporary America.

When we argue endlessly about tax policy, we put the cart before the horse. The purpose of government, beyond keeping order, is investing in and insuring its citizens; taxes are the means for financing government's actual business. Our preoccupation with theprogressiveincome tax in particular is self-limiting and self-defeating: the right response to growing income inequality is to ensure that we enjoy a  progressive fiscal system. Not particularly progressive tax systems can fund very progressive overall fiscal systems, once the distribution of the benefits of government spending are taken into account.

The book bursts two fundamentally narcissistic fiscal policy bubbles. First, itdelivers“our dismal report card,”to puncture the simplistic belief in American exceptionalism in all matters, all the time. The book employes cross country comparisons across a wide array of metrics to demonstrate that contemporary American society does not advance Americans’welfare as systematically as we pretend.

Second, it dismantles the ethos of  “market triumphalism”—the worldview that perfect markets yield perfect happiness, and that government invariably stands between us and those perfect markets. It does so in part by contrasting the actual beliefs of Adam Smith, "moralist and mensch,"  with the cartoon cheerleader for laissez faire outcomes paraded by market triumphalists. We Are Better Than This emphasizes the contingent nature of many outcomes that we like to believe are the result solely of our inner merit, and demonstrates that social insurance in particular can encourage economically productive risk-taking.

We Are Better Than Thisrelies extensively on  recent research studies and objective data to support its arguments.  It is sensitive to issues like the efficiency costs of taxation, but dispels the myth that low tax rates are the only ambrosia that can attract and nurture the Growth Fairy. It offers a specific tax plan to finance the government we need. And finally, the book explains in an accessible way important budget, political and economic concepts, so that readers for example learn how GDP does not measure our income, much less our welfare, or how different measures of income inequality are constructed.

 When we choose how government should spend and tax, we open a window into our "fiscal soul,” because those choices are the means by which we express the values we cherish and the regard in which we hold our fellow citizens. Our fiscal soul today is in peril;  We Are Better Than This shows how we can reclaim it, to the greater material prosperity and happiness of all Americans.
